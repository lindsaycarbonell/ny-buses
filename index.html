Parents Frustrated Over School Bus Delays

By Lindsay Carbonell and Felipe De La Hoz May 26, 2017

Most weekday mornings, Bronx resident Rosalind Brown wakes up four times. Her multiple alarms ring at intervals so she can sleep between prodding her 13-year-old son, Jalen, to get ready for school. The school bus arrives at 6:05 a.m. to take her seventh-grader to school in Harlem by 7:45 a.m.

But Jalen, who was diagnosed with Attention Deficit Disorder, often arrives at Opportunity Charter School at 9 a.m. or later, Ms. Brown said. And there have been days he has missed the first two periods of school.

“The bus is late, and you have children in there who are missing E.L.A., and math, and science,” said Ms. Brown, referring to English language arts.

For Jalen, like other students with disabilities, taking public transportation is not always feasible. Navigating subway lines and bus transfers requires focus that many children with learning disabilities may not have. Also, 75 percent of subway stations do not have an elevator.  

Jalen Brown attends Opportunity Charter School, which has the most recorded bus delays in the Institute's central Harlem coverage area.

Ms. Brown recalled an incident where Jalen, confused by the directions in a subway station, handed his cash and MetroCard to a stranger. “Nothing happened, but imagine if they’d taken his cash and MetroCard? How would he have gotten home?” she said.

New York City’s school bus drivers have reported more than 135,000 delays since September 2015. Their buses are not owned or operated by any government entity. The Department of Education's Office of Pupil Transportation has contracts with about 90 private vendors to ferry about 600,000 students to schools in New York City and neighboring counties. The companies don’t change much; open bids to drive a school route in the city happened in 1979 and in 2013.

In Community Board 10 in Central Harlem, there were 692 school bus delays from Sept. 2, 2015 to May 21, 2017, according to data recorded by the Department of Education’s Office of Pupil Transportation. Of the 35 public schools and charter schools in that area, 28 reported at least one school bus delay, the data showed.

Opportunity Charter School, which has grades sixth through 12th, had 132 reported bus delays, nearly twice as many as the next highest school, P.S. 241, which had 79 delays, in that nearly 20-month period.

About 460 students attended Opportunity Charter School in the 2015-16 school year, and more than half, or 55 percent, had a disability, according to the New York State Education Department’s data portal. Many of the students rely on school buses, often because their disability requires special attention at school, which can be farther away from their home. Like Jaden, many of the students at the charter school travel from the Bronx. 

School bus delays can have a snowball effect when it comes to learning.

 “Many kids have a hard time transitioning from one environment to another. Even if they get to school a half hour late, it may take them an hour to get used to the environment,” said Lori Podvesker, an administrator at a disability advocacy group called INCLUDEnyc. She has a 14-year-old son with cerebral palsy who rides the bus from Brooklyn to his school in Manhattan.

The Department of Education and Amalgamated Transit Union Local 1181, which represents drivers servicing about half of routes, did not respond to requests for comment.

Jason Maymon, a spokesman for Opportunity Charter School, said the school had problems with bus operators in the past, but it did not “have any problems” with its current operator. He would not give details or elaborate.

The Office of Pupil Transportation operates a complaint and information hotline for parents to get information on school bus status and lodge complaints about bus service. But parents and advocates say that it provides vague information on expected arrivals and offers little value.

“When you’re talking to someone on the phone, you’re not speaking to anyone with any power to change the routes or put another bus out there,” said Sara Catalinotto, president of Parents to Improve School Transportation, which is pushing for the city to take control of school buses.

Ms. Brown is considering pulling her son out of Opportunity Charter School after two years. “I put him in that school because the school has that ratio of kids with disabilities. At that school he receives the services that he needs.”
